QUANTUM MECHANICS

Electrons and other subatomic particles behave as both particles and waves. An individual particle like an electron has mass, but it is spread out, not located in one position, and it has a wavelength. The wavelike nature of electrons and other subatomic particles, as well as the paradox described by Heisenberg‚Äôs uncertainty principle, made it impossible to use the equations of classical physics to describe the motion of electrons in atoms. Scientists needed a new approach that took the wave behavior of the electron into account. In 1926, an Austrian physicist, Erwin Schr√∂dinger (1887‚Äì1961; Nobel Prize in Physics, 1933), developed wave mechanics, a mathematical technique that describes the relationship between the motion of a particle that exhibits wavelike properties and its allowed energies. Schr√∂dinger's wave equation allowed scientists to make predictions about the electronic structure of atoms. Where are the electrons found? What is their energy? Although quantum mechanics uses sophisticated mathematics, you do not need to understand the mathematical details to follow our discussion of its general conclusions. We focus on the properties of the wavefunctions that are the solutions of Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs equations.

WAVEFUNCTIONS

A wavefunction (Œ®) is a mathematical function that relates the location of an electron and the energy of the electron. A wavefunction uses three variables to describe the position of an electron in space (as with the Cartesian coordinates x, y, and z). A fourth variable specifies the time at which the electron is at the specified location. By analogy, if you were the captain of a ship trying to intercept an enemy submarine, you would need to know its latitude, longitude, and depth, as well as the time at which it was going to be at this position (Figure 1.1.1 ). For electrons, we can ignore the time dependence because we will be using standing waves, which by definition do not change with time, to describe the position of an electron.

The magnitude of the wavefunction at a particular point in space is proportional to the amplitude of the wavefunction at that point. Many wavefunctions are complex functions . Hence the amplitude of the wave has no real physical significance.In contrast, the sign of the wavefunction (either positive or negative) corresponds to the phase of the wave, which will be important in our discussion of chemical bonding. The sign of the wavefunction should not be confused with a positive or negative electrical charge. The square of the wavefunction at a given point in space is proportional to the probability of finding an electron at that point in space. The square of the wavefunction (Œ®2 ) is always a real quantity. The probability of finding an electron at any point in space depends on several factors, including the distance from the nucleus and, in many cases, the atomic equivalent of latitude and longitude. One way of graphically representing the probability distribution is demonstrated in Figure 1.1.2 for a hydrogen atom. The probability of finding an electron is indicated by the density of colored dots.Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs approach treats electrons as three-dimensional standing waves. It is a requirement of standing waves that they be in phase with one another to avoid cancellation; this results in a limited number of solutions (wavefunctions), each of which is associated with a particular energy. As in Bohr‚Äôs model, the energy of an electron in an atom is quantized; it can have only certain allowed values, which are specified by the quantum numbers. Quantum numbers define solutions to the Schr√∂dinger equation. The major difference between Bohr‚Äôs model and Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs approach is that Bohr had to impose the idea of quantization arbitrarily, whereas in Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs approach, quantization is a natural consequence of describing an electron as a standing wave.

APPLICATIONS

1. Quantum mechanics uses wave functions to describe the behavior of particles at the atomic level.
2. Wave functions play a key role in quantum computing by describing the states of qubits.
3. They are utilized in medical imaging techniques like MRI and ultrasound for imaging the human body.
4. In optics, wave functions help to understand how light behaves through different media.
5. In acoustics, wave functions are used to model sound waves for designing speakers and microphones.
6. Semiconductor devices like transistors rely on wave functions to predict electron movement.
7. In astronomy, wave functions are used to study cosmic phenomena such as black holes.
8. Material science uses wave functions to predict the properties of new materials at the atomic level.
9. Engineers use wave functions to simulate and design electronic and photonic devices.

VISUALISATIONS

1. Probabilistic Interpretation: Visualizing a wave function allows you to see the probability distribution of a particle‚Äôs position, helping you understand where the particle is likely to be found.
2. Understanding Quantum States: Wave function visualizations represent quantum states of particles. By plotting the wave function, we can observe how particles evolve in time and space.
3. Superposition and Interference: Visualizing wave functions helps in understanding quantum phenomena like superposition and interference, where multiple wave functions combine to form new patterns.
4. Time Evolution of Wave Functions: Animations of wave function evolution over time show how quantum systems change, providing insight into concepts like quantum tunneling or particle-wave duality.
5. Node and Antinode Representation: In standing waves, visualizations show the locations of nodes (where the wave function is zero) and antinodes (where it reaches maximum amplitude), crucial for understanding resonance in systems like strings or particles in a box.
6. Energy Levels: The wave function‚Äôs visualization in quantum mechanics helps to illustrate different energy levels in a system. For instance, electron energy levels in atoms can be understood by examining the shapes of the wave functions at different quantum states.
7. Wave Function Normalization: Graphical representations of wave functions can help visualize normalization, where the total probability (integral of the squared wave function) is made equal to one.
8. Boundary Conditions and Constraints: Visualizations help show how boundary conditions affect wave functions, like how the wave function behaves at the edges of a potential well or at infinity, which is crucial for solving problems like the particle in a box.
9. Visualization of Orbital Shapes: For atoms, visualizing wave functions provides a clear picture of the electron orbitals in an atom (such as s, p, d orbitals), helping us understand electron density distributions.
10. Comparing Different Wave Functions: By plotting multiple wave functions with different parameters (such as different quantum numbers or potential well depths), visualizations allow easy comparison of how these changes affect the system's behavior.

1. Potential Energy The particle is confined within the box and cannot exist outside it because the potential is infinite. Therefore, the probability of finding the particle outside the box is zero.

The energy levels depend on the size of the box: The energy is inversely proportional to the square of the box size ùêø L. The larger the box, the lower the energy levels.
